# Outreach Templates

> Last updated: February 16, 2026
> These are starting frameworks. Personalize every message. Track what converts and evolve.

---

## Principles

1. **Never pitch.** Offer insight. The goal is to start a conversation, not close a deal.
2. **Be specific.** Reference something real — their product, their raise, a public post.
3. **Keep it short.** Under 100 words for cold outreach. Respect their time.
4. **One ask.** Don't offer three things. Offer one thing that's obviously valuable.
5. **No jargon.** "Your codebase might have scaling risks" not "we provide comprehensive technical due diligence services."

## Template 1: Post-Raise Founder (Cold Email / LinkedIn DM)

**Context:** They just raised. They built fast. They're about to scale.

**When to use:** Within 2–4 weeks of a public funding announcement.

```
Subject: Quick note on scaling after your raise

Hey [Name],

Congrats on the [round size] raise — [one specific thing about their product
that shows you actually looked].

We work with post-raise startups whose codebases were built fast
(often with AI tools) and now need to hold up as the team and
traffic scale. The pattern we see: what got you to launch won't
get you to Series [next round] without some architectural attention.

We offer a fixed-scope code health check ($1,500–$2,500 depending
on complexity) — no ongoing commitment, just a clear picture of
where the risks are.

Worth a look? Happy to send over what the output looks like.

— Vipul
Variant Systems
```

**Personalization hooks:**
- Mention their specific product or feature
- Reference their tech stack if visible (job postings, GitHub, blog)
- Note if they're hiring engineers (signal: they know they need help)

## Template 2: Founder with a Broken Product (Reddit / Community Response)

**Context:** Someone posts about their app breaking, struggling with tech debt, or looking for help fixing AI-generated code.

**When to use:** Organic response in r/webdev, r/SaaS, r/startups, Indie Hackers, etc.

```
This sounds like a classic case of [specific problem they described].

A few things I'd check first:
- [Specific technical suggestion relevant to their post]
- [Second suggestion]

We see this pattern a lot with AI-generated codebases — the code
works initially but hits a wall when you try to [scale / add features /
handle edge cases]. The root cause is usually [architectural issue],
not the individual bugs.

If you want a second set of eyes, we do fixed-scope code health
checks. Happy to chat — no pressure either way.
```

**Key rules:**
- Lead with genuine help, not a pitch
- Be technically specific (not vague platitudes)
- Don't mention Variant Systems in the first response — earn the right
- Follow up via DM only if they engage

## Template 3: CTO / VP Engineering at Growing Company (LinkedIn)

**When to use:** After connecting on LinkedIn, engaging with their content, or finding them through industry discussions.

compounds. We help engineering teams audit AI-generated
codebases before that wall hits.

Would it be useful if I shared what the common failure patterns
look like? No pitch — just the patterns we're seeing.

— Vipul

**Context:** Founder posting on job boards, Reddit, or communities looking for someone to fix or improve their app.

**When to use:** When the post describes rescue/fix work, not greenfield development.

```
Hey [Name],

Before you hire someone full-time for this, it might be worth
getting a code health check first. A lot of the time what looks
like "we need a developer to fix bugs" is actually an
architectural issue that a new hire will inherit and struggle with.

We do fixed-scope audits that map out exactly what's wrong,
what's risky, and what to fix first — so whoever does the work
(us or someone else) has a clear roadmap.

Takes about [X] days and costs $1,500–$2,500 depending on
codebase size. Happy to share a sample report if useful.
```

## Channel-Specific Notes

**LinkedIn:** Keep DMs under 75 words. Engage with their content 2–3 times before DMing. Never pitch in comments.

**Email:** Subject line is everything. Keep it under 6 words. No "Introduction" or "Partnership Opportunity."

**Reddit/Communities:** Help first, sell never. Your post history IS your pitch. Be the person who gives useful answers about AI code quality.

**Twitter/X:** Quote-tweet with insight, don't reply with sales pitches. Build reputation through threads and takes, not outreach.
